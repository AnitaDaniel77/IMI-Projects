Lesson 9 — Types of Digital Design

Design terminology has grown a lot. Before going deeper into UX and UI, it helps to know where they sit within the broader design spectrum.

The Design Spectrum

Four types of design, moving from analog to digital:

Service Design

All-encompassing. Takes into account every point of contact between a company and its customers — analog and digital. In a restaurant, this covers everything from the reservation call to the bill at the end of the meal.

Customer Experience Design (CX)

Concerned with the customer's experience with a particular brand or service. Designs all interactions between the service provider and the customer.

User Experience Design (UX)

Focuses on all interactions between the user and a specific touchpoint. Mainly digital but covers the full experience across all touchpoints. UX is NOT about visuals — it is about the overall feel of the experience.

User Interface Design (UI)

Pixel-perfect design of digital interfaces — usability, conversion, look and feel. UI provides the visual solution to the problems UX identifies.

Real life example

Capitec customers can't easily send money via the app. That is the user problem. The bank wants more customers using the app and increasing their loyalty. That is the business goal. Good UX/UI design is the solution sitting in the middle of those two needs.

User-Centered Design (UCD)

An iterative process where the needs, wants and limitations of the end user are considered at every stage of product development. The user is king. Keeping them at the centre of every decision means you understand what they want, what they don't want, and how they will interact with each element of your product.

Design Thinking

Design thinking is a process for solving creative problems. A process gives creativity structure — we are actually more creative with limitations than with a blank canvas of endless possibilities.

The five steps — same as the UX process, and iterative:

1. Empathize

Relate to the problem. Understand the user deeply enough to develop solutions that genuinely improve their lives. Example user thought: "This is taking too long to figure out."

2. Define

Analyse and synthesise your observations into a clear problem statement. Example: "As a user, I want to understand the product offering so I can make an informed decision quickly."

3. Ideate

With a solid problem statement in hand, generate ideas. Hundreds of techniques exist — sketching, mind mapping, brainstorming. Get creative.

4. Prototype

Turn prioritised ideas into something tangible — pen on paper, wireframes, rough sketches, or a full high-fidelity prototype.

5. Test

Get the prototype in front of real users. Watch them complete tasks. Observe, empathise with their choices, and feed what you learn back into the process.

Key takeaway

Design thinking is a toolbox, not a rigid checklist. As you gain experience you will learn which steps to reach for depending on the problem. For now, follow all five in order and iterate at each stage.
