Lesson 5 — UX Goals and Nielsen's Usability Components

The Little Lemon ordering and reservation problems are usability issues, not just design issues. To fix them you need to align the customer's goals with Adrian's business goals.

What is usability?

Usability gauges how intuitive or easy a product is to use. Jacob Nielsen — a highly recognised professional in the field — breaks usability down into five quality components.

Nielsen's 5 Usability Components

1. Learnability

How easy is it for users to complete a task the first time they try?

Little Lemon example: a first-time customer should be able to place a delivery order without any guidance or confusion.

2. Efficiency

Once users have learned the design, how quickly can they complete tasks?

Little Lemon example: if a customer wants to change their order, can they do it quickly without starting over?

3. Memorability

If a user leaves and comes back, how easily can they pick up where they left off?

Little Lemon example: a customer gets distracted mid-order — can they find their place again without frustration?

4. Errors

How many mistakes do users make, how severe are they, and can they recover easily?

Good design prevents errors before they happen and provides clear solutions when they do.

5. Satisfaction

Is the product pleasant and enjoyable to use? Satisfaction is hard to measure but easy to feel — you know when a product just works.

Experience goals — desirable vs undesirable

Users go through a range of emotions when using a product. Design for the desirable ones and actively remove the undesirable ones.

Desirable

  • Enjoyable and fun
  • Relaxing and satisfying
  • Engaging and motivating
  • Intuitive — users should not have to think too hard

Undesirable

  • Slow and confusing
  • Complicated to navigate
  • Boring, frustrating, or stressful

Real life example

Think about the Capitec app. When you open it and the screen goes blank with no message, that breaks learnability, memorability and satisfaction all at once. A simple error state — "Something went wrong. Tap to retry." — would fix three usability components in one line of text.

Designing for real user needs

Good design is helpful without being patronising. Example: a customer using crutches wants to reserve a table near the door. Can your design support that requirement? If not, can they find that information easily enough?

The goal is for users to feel supported and empowered, not second-guessed.

Key takeaway

Consider Nielsen's five components and your users' experience goals from the very start of the design process. Solving for usability early prevents bigger problems later.
