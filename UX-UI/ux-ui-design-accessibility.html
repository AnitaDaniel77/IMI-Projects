Lesson 14 — Designing for Accessibility

Accessibility enables people with disabilities to interpret, understand, navigate, interact with and contribute to the web. We don't intentionally exclude users — we often just don't know what we don't know. These guidelines meet Section 508 and WCAG 2.0 minimum standards.

Design for a wide range of users: people who are blind, colour blind or have poor eyesight, those who are deaf or have poor hearing, people with permanent or temporary mobility impairments and people with cognitive disabilities. Also consider young, old, power users, casual users and everyone in between.

Accessibility Guidelines

1. Don't use colour alone to convey information

1 in 12 men and 1 in 200 women are colour blind. Colour can be used to highlight information but must never be the only way to communicate it. Use additional identifiers — asterisks for required form fields, labels and icons alongside colour on graphs.

2. Provide sufficient contrast between foreground and background

WCAG recommends a minimum contrast ratio of 4.5:1 for text against its background. This drops to 3:1 if the font is at least 24px or 19px bold. Applies to text on images, gradient backgrounds and buttons — not logos or incidental text in photos.

3. Ensure interactive elements are easily identifiable

Links and buttons should have distinct styles. Change appearance on hover, keyboard focus and touch activation. Some users cannot use a mouse and rely entirely on their keyboard — all interactive elements must be keyboard accessible. Use a visible border or highlight to show keyboard focus as the user tabs through the page.

4. Ensure form elements include clearly associated labels

Every field needs a descriptive label except checkboxes and radio buttons. Labels should sit left of or above the field. Avoid too much space between labels and their fields — proximity communicates relationship.

5. Make navigation options clear and consistent

Navigation should be consistent in naming, styling and positioning across all pages. Offer more than one way to navigate — site search, site map, breadcrumbs and clear headings all help users know where they are.

6. Provide easily identifiable feedback

Confirm form submissions clearly. When something goes wrong, display the error where the user can easily see it with clear instructions for how to fix it. Important feedback that requires user action must not be hidden or easy to miss.

7. Use headings and spacing to group related content

Whitespace and proximity communicate relationships between content. Headings reduce clutter and make pages easier to scan. If two things belong together, they should look like they belong together.

8. Design for different viewport sizes

Consider how content displays on mobile phones and zoomed browser windows. Header and navigation placement may need to change at smaller sizes. Check that text size and line width are set for maximum readability.

9. Provide controls for content that starts automatically

Any auto-playing animation, carousel, background music or video must have visible controls so users can stop it. Not everyone wants movement on a page and some users find it actively harmful.

Real life example

The Little Lemon sign-up form that threw a validation error only after submission fails at least three of these guidelines — no inline feedback, no clear error identification, and no guidance on how to fix the problem. A user relying on a screen reader would be completely lost.

Key takeaway

Accessibility constraints are not limitations on creativity — they are prompts for better ideas. Designing inclusively produces better products for every user, not just those with disabilities.
