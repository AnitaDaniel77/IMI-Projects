Lesson 36 — Design Elements and Principles

Lines

Lines include almost everything — even the letters you're reading are made of curved, angled and straight lines. Straight lines convey order and neatness, wavy lines suggest movement, zig-zagged lines suggest tension or excitement. Leading lines direct the viewer's eye through a design to specific areas.

Scale

Scale is the deliberate sizing of elements. It doesn't have to be realistic — sizing elements dramatically signals which parts of the design are more or less important.

Colour

Colour creates moods, atmospheres and emotions. Each shade carries its own connotations. It can make or break a design.

Repetition

Repetition keeps branding consistent and ties design elements together. That's why you instantly recognise a brand's logo, tone and colour scheme — you've seen it repeated enough times to remember it.

Negative space

The space between or around elements that forms its own shape. Used strategically, negative space creates clever and stunning designs.

Symmetry

Humans are drawn to symmetry — it signals balance and order. Perfect symmetry isn't always possible, but introducing subtle symmetrical elements improves the feel of a layout.

Transparency

Also called opacity. Lower opacity makes elements lighter and less noticeable. Higher opacity makes them solid and prominent.

Texture

Texture adds tactility and depth to a design, making it stand out and feel distinctive.

Balance

No element should overpower another. Think of each element as having a weight — consider its size, shape and visual weight relative to everything else on the page.

Hierarchy

Headings command the most attention. Subheadings and pull quotes are next. Body copy and links are at the bottom. Images follow the same rule — larger, more colourful and central elements have higher hierarchy than smaller or duller ones.

Contrast

The degree of difference between two elements — dark vs light, thick vs thin, large vs small. Contrast improves readability and draws attention to specific elements like call-to-action buttons. Use it to make designs pop.

Frame

Framing draws attention to specific elements and directs the eye. Box outlines or graphic elements can frame and highlight important content.

Grid

The foundation of any design. Rows and columns that keep content organised, aligned, legible and visually consistent.

Direction

Also called flow — how the eye moves across the page. Research shows eyes commonly follow an F, E or Z pattern. The eye naturally starts top-left and works down to bottom-right. Place your most important content top-left or along the top.

Depth

Techniques for creating the illusion of depth in a flat medium:

  • Shadows — observe real shadows to understand how light hits objects
  • Overlapping elements — reduces flatness and creates layering
  • Perspective — gives a 3D effect by adjusting an element's viewpoint
  • Flat design — the opposite, intentionally minimal depth

Composition

The overall arrangement of all elements in a design. This is where all the other principles come together. A clear message is not just about the content — it's about how the content is arranged to deliver that message clearly.
