Lesson 35 — Design Elements: Images, Colour and Shapes

Design elements are like ingredients in a recipe. Every component of a visual message matters and how they are combined determines how the design is interpreted. The main elements are line, text, colour, shape, graphic elements and space.

Line

The most fundamental design element. Lines come in different colours, sizes and styles. They can direct the eye to a specific area. Bold thick lines catch attention, thin lines are subtle. Visible or invisible, lines create structure and flow.

Type

Web typography must work across all screen sizes. Five things must be balanced for comfortable reading: typeface, type size, colour, line height and letter shapes.

Colour

Colour sets mood, builds brand identity and evokes emotion. Digital design uses the RGB colour system — red, green and blue combined in different amounts to produce a broad range of colours.

Colour qualities:

  • Hue — how closely a colour relates to the primary colours (red, orange, yellow, green, blue, indigo, violet). "Blue-green" uses two hues to describe one colour.
  • Tint — a colour mixed with white to make it lighter
  • Tone — a colour mixed with grey to mute it
  • Shade — a colour mixed with black to darken it
  • Saturation — the intensity of a colour. Low saturation looks faded, high saturation looks rich and deep.

Shape

Shapes draw attention and add meaning to a layout. Three types:

  • Geometric — precise and structured. Squares, circles, triangles.
  • Organic — smooth, natural, no sharp edges. Give layouts a softer emphasis.
  • Abstract — simplified representations of reality. A stick figure is abstract. Most logos use abstract shapes to represent a brand's spirit.

Images

Images are the most accessible and engaging medium. Used well, they draw attention, direct the eye, evoke feeling and build trust. A variety of media elements improves the overall user experience.

Space

Space is the area a shape occupies and the background against which it sits. Two types:

  • Positive space — the area of interest, the actual element
  • Negative space — the space surrounding it

Narrow spacing links elements to show they are connected. Wider gaps separate elements to show they are distinct. Overlapping elements strengthens their relationship further.

Real life example

Little Lemon uses yellow (#F4CE14) as its primary brand colour — high saturation, warm hue. It signals energy and appetite. The dark green (#495E57) as background creates strong contrast. Both colour choices are deliberate and reinforce the Mediterranean brand identity.
