Lesson 13 — Design Principles in Detail

Dieter Rams — 10 Principles of Good Design

1. Good design is innovative

Every change, improvement or new idea is an opportunity to innovate. Innovation does not always mean starting from scratch — improving what exists counts too.

2. Good design makes a product useful

Products are not art to be admired. They exist to serve a purpose and solve a problem. A stunning product that is unusable is worthless.

3. Good design is aesthetic

Beautiful things make us feel comfortable and more inclined to use them. That said, utility should never be sacrificed for beauty.

4. Good design makes a product understandable

A well-designed product is self-explanatory. It communicates what it is, what it is for and how to use it — without an instruction manual.

Bad example: a door handle that does not indicate push or pull. A tap that does not indicate hot or cold.

5. Good design is unobtrusive

Over-designed products get in the user's way. The best designs let users do exactly what they want to do without the design calling attention to itself.

6. Good design is honest

Good design does not manipulate. It does not pretend to be something it is not or make promises it cannot keep. It always has the user's best interests in mind.

7. Good design is long-lasting

Avoid being fashionable. A design that chases trends will look dated quickly. Timeless design never appears old or antiquated.

8. Good design is thorough down to the last detail

Nothing is left to chance. Every element must earn its place. Products should be designed with care and concern for the intended user.

9. Good design is environmentally friendly

Design should not be wasteful — physically or digitally. Save resources wherever possible throughout a product's lifespan.

10. Good design is as little design as possible

Less clutter means more clarity. Anything that does not serve the user should be removed. Intentional minimalism, not laziness.

Jakob Nielsen — 10 Usability Heuristics

1. Visibility of system status

Always keep users informed about what is happening. Battery level, Wi-Fi signal, a loading spinner — these all communicate system status. Without them users feel insecure.

2. Match between system and the real world

Use language and concepts familiar to the user, not internal jargon. Meet users where they are mentally.

3. User control and freedom

Users make mistakes. Provide clear emergency exits — undo, redo, back buttons — so they can recover without going through an extended process.

4. Consistency and standards

A submit button should look the same on every page. Information presented in a particular format on one page should match every other page. Consistency builds trust.

5. Error prevention

Prevent errors before they happen rather than relying on good error messages after the fact. Remove conditions that cause errors and confirm risky actions before they are taken.

Little Lemon example: the sign-up form threw a validation error only after submission. Inline validation would have prevented the user abandoning the task.

6. Recognition rather than recall

Do not make users remember things between steps. Surface the information they need, when they need it. Clear navigation, hints and notifications all reduce memory load.

7. Flexibility and efficiency of use

Design for both new and experienced users. A beginner should be able to find their way without shortcuts. An expert should be able to use shortcuts to move fast.
