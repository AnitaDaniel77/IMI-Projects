Lesson 40 — Design Systems Deep Dive

A design system is a constantly evolving collection of reusable components, principles and guidelines that give designers and developers a common language for consistent product design. It contains both tangible elements like components and patterns, and intangible ones like brand values and shared ways of working.

Two core parts

Reusable elements

The practical building blocks — components, patterns, guidelines and tools. Advice on how to apply elements together: the why and the how.

Shared values

The beliefs and goals that align the team. These change over time and any changes must be communicated widely. Shared values unite designers, developers and stakeholders around a common mindset.

What to include — start with who, not what

The first question when building a design system is not "what should I include?" but "who will use it and how?" Then ask: what works and what doesn't, what is the team's experience, and what tools already exist? The answers will show you where to start.

Design principles

Guiding sentences that help teams achieve the product's goal through design. Google Material Design is a well-known example. Their principles are: material is the metaphor, it is bold, graphic and intentional, motion provides meaning. Simple but they inform every decision made within that system.

Brand identity and language

The visible elements of a brand — colour, design, logo — need rules that act as grammar for the system. Dos and don'ts and "good examples" ensure teams use the right combinations and keep the brand recognisable and distinct.

Components and patterns

Components are the blocks — reusable UI elements with technical and functional documentation explaining how they work and how users interact with them. Patterns are the building instructions — recommendations for how to use components logically and consistently across all products.

The distinction: a component tells you what it is and how it works. A pattern tells you when and how to use it.

Key takeaway

A well-implemented design system educates team members, streamlines work and frees designers to focus on complex UX problems rather than reinventing basics. A poorly maintained one becomes an unwieldy collection of components nobody uses. The people who manage it matter as much as the system itself.
