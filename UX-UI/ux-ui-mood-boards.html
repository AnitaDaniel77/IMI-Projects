Lesson 38 — Mood Boards in Design

Mood boards are graphic panels that combine photos, colours, illustrations, animations, fonts and textures to create a visual guide for creative decisions. They make abstract concepts like emotions and desires concrete and shareable.

What mood boards are used for

  • Documenting research — benchmarking, persona definition, UI ideation
  • Designer exploration — a judgment-free space to explore ideas early
  • Combining visions — different stakeholders contribute and reach agreement on the project's direction

Physical vs digital

Digital boards are easier to put together, accept animations and GIFs, and can collect large amounts of data from the web. Physical boards work well in office environments as a constant visual reminder, and allow tactile elements like fabrics, paints and materials that lose meaning when reproduced digitally. For web and app projects, digital is recommended.

How to create a mood board

Recognise the goal first. Write a clear statement about which emotion should be triggered when a user encounters your product. Example: "Tilly must feel motivated to order food when she browses the Little Lemon website."

Set the tone with keywords that represent the team's vision. For Little Lemon: "fresh", "Mediterranean", "traditional". Pin these to the board so they're always visible.

Collect information before hunting for visuals. Take notes from meetings, user interviews and research. Let the data inform the references you choose.

Establish values — define three or four adjectives that guide reference selection. Modern, fun, colourful, traditional. These keep the board focused.

Think big first, then small. Start with broad themes and narrow down. Divide the board into smaller clusters based on similar patterns and remove anything that doesn't fit.

Choose wisely. If two references say the same thing, keep one. Everything on the board should earn its place and move the project in a clear direction.

Key takeaway

A mood board unites the team around a shared vision before any design decisions are made. It's a reference point that keeps creative work aligned from the first sketch to the final interface.
