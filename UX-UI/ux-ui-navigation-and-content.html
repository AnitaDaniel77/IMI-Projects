Lesson 21 — Navigation Best Practices and Content Strategy

Tilly's feedback "I have no idea where I am" points to two things: how content is organised and how navigation communicates structure. Two methodologies address this — content strategy and information architecture.

Content strategy

Content strategy is about presenting content in a way that promotes the product and aligns business goals with user needs. Four things to think about:

  • Prioritisation — what content matters most to your users and why
  • Organisation — how you group, label and relate content so users can find what they need
  • Presentation — how content pieces are assembled into what the user actually sees
  • Specifications — the requirements for each individual content piece

Information architecture

Information architecture structures and indexes content to support good navigation. For a site the size of Little Lemon, a card sorting exercise works well. Write every element you want on the site on sticky notes, then ask real users to group them into categories that make sense to them. The groups they create become your navigation sections.

Little Lemon navigation structure

Based on card sorting, the site organises into five primary sections:

  • Home — introductory summary of the site
  • About — produce, ingredients, the brothers, grandmother's story, social links
  • Menu — up-to-date printable traditional restaurant menu
  • Reservations — table reservations
  • Order Online — food delivery orders

This primary navigation bar sits at the top of every page. Having it on every page reinforces the site structure and gives users a consistent sense of place.

Navigation design rules applied

  • Navigation has room to breathe — no clutter around it
  • Contrasting colours so links stand out from the background
  • Links look like links — not hidden in body text like on the old site
  • Active page is visually distinct — heavier font weight, different colour or underline
  • The logo links back to the homepage
  • A footer repeats links to all pages and social channels

Mobile

On mobile, screen space means the full nav can't show. A hamburger menu replaces it — tapping opens a side menu with links to all pages. This is the familiar pattern mobile users already know.
