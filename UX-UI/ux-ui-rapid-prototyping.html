Lesson 31 — Rapid Prototyping

Before investing time and money building a product, you need to know it works. A prototype is a scaled-down version you test on real users to catch usability problems and design flaws before they make it into development.

What is rapid prototyping?

Rapid prototyping is the process of creating a fast, preliminary version that closely resembles the final product, testing it, and iterating based on feedback. The term comes from manufacturing where physical 3D models were built and tested before mass production. Digital designers adopted the same idea — tools like Figma let you stitch screens together in minutes, making all digital prototyping effectively rapid.

Advantages

A prototype gives everyone something concrete to react to. Describing a product and showing it are completely different things — descriptions fail with stakeholders and users who don't share your mental model. A rapid prototype removes that miscommunication.

Catching problems early is cheaper than catching them in development. Testing continuously and quickly validates that you are building something people actually want, not what you assumed they wanted.

The rapid prototyping cycle

Build what you want to test. Test it on users, stakeholders or your team. Make changes based on feedback. Repeat. That's it — simple but powerful.

You don't need to prototype the entire product at once. Focus on one section at a time. The two most valuable things to prototype are:

  • Main functions and features — where users spend most of their time
  • New interactions and patterns — anything that deviates from what users already expect, like a new checkout flow or an unusual login screen

Fidelity levels

Low-fidelity prototypes (paper sketches, rough wireframes) help users focus on whether the flow works. Is the concept right? Use these early.

Mid-fidelity prototypes (wireframes with basic interactivity) validate element and screen behaviour. Users focus on whether interactions make sense.

High-fidelity prototypes closely resemble the finished product — built in Figma with real colours, fonts and images. Users tend to focus on visual design at this stage, so these are better suited to later in the process when you want to validate visual decisions rather than structure.

Key takeaway

The right fidelity level depends on what stage you're at and what you want to learn. Start low, test fast, iterate, and move up in fidelity as the design gets more solid.
