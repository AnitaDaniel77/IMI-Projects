Lesson 25 — Typography and Best Practices

Over 90% of online information is text. Getting typography wrong doesn't just look bad — it makes content unreadable, which means users leave.

Key terms

A typeface (font family) is the whole system — Arial, Times New Roman, Comic Sans. A font is one specific weight or style within that system, the way a track belongs to an album. They're not the same thing, even though people use them interchangeably.

Baseline: the invisible line text sits on. Cap height: how tall flat capitals like M and H are. X-height: the height of lowercase letters like x and z — typefaces with large x-heights have less breathing room between lines, which affects legibility. Ascenders are the parts of letters that rise above the x-height (the stem on a b or d). Descenders drop below the baseline (g, p, y).

Tracking is consistent spacing applied across all characters in a block of text. Kerning is the adjustment between two specific characters. Leading is the vertical space between lines.

Serif fonts have small strokes at the ends of letterforms that guide the eye across a line — traditionally easier to read in print. Sans-serif fonts drop those strokes and have historically worked better on lower-resolution screens, though that distinction matters less as screen resolution improves.

Using typography to improve UX

Hierarchy is the most immediate lever. Headings are larger and heavier, body text is smaller and lighter, and the contrast between them tells users where to look first. Start by setting a comfortable body text size, then build up from there. For desktop, 16px minimum for body text. iOS, 11pt minimum. Android, 14pt minimum.

Line length affects how tiring text is to read. A simple formula: multiply font size by 30 to get the ideal line length in pixels. At 10px font size, that's 300px max line width.

Text colour needs contrast against its background — not just for aesthetics but for accessibility. Use different colours to signal hierarchy: one for headings, one for body, one for links, one for error states. Don't make users guess which text is interactive.

Avoid walls of text. A solid block of copy with no breaks, no images, no subheadings — the grey wall effect — kills readability. Break it up with paragraph spacing, headings, images or pull quotes at reasonable intervals.

White space between text blocks isn't wasted space. It reduces cognitive load and makes content easier to scan.

One thing to remember

You can follow every rule above and still miss something a real user notices immediately. Typography needs user testing, not just rule-following.
