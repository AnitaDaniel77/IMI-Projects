Lesson 33 — Usability Test Cheat Sheet

Usability testing can be done during design, development or after launch. Two broad methods: user interviews and unmoderated usability testing.

User Interviews

Before the interview

  • Discussion guide — research questions prepared in advance
  • Interview schedule
  • Roles — notetakers, moderators, translators
  • Scenarios list
  • Test materials — prototypes, competitor websites
  • Consent and disclosure forms
  • Exit survey

Types of questions to ask

Warm-up — get the participant comfortable and talking. "Do you prefer coffee or tea?" Something low-stakes.

Open questions — broad and exploratory. "Explain your experience with..." "How do you..." "When was the last time you..."

Task-based — "Show us how you do it." "What motivates you to do that."

Recall — "Tell me everything you remember about the last time you used..." "What was the most noteworthy aspect of..."

Beliefs and attitudes — "What are your thoughts on..." "What do you enjoy most about..." "What do you despise most..."

Inquiry — dig deeper into anything interesting. "Tell me more about that." "Why did you go about it that way." "Could you elaborate?"

Closing — "Is there anything else you'd like to say?"

After the interview

Debrief — what went well, what can be improved. Prepare a topline summary to keep stakeholders updated on findings and help with early decisions.

Unmoderated Usability Testing

No facilitator present — participants use the product alone. Can be in-person at a set location or remote via an internet platform. Remote is more common as it removes time and location constraints.

9 steps to run an unmoderated test

1. Define objectives — what do stakeholders want to learn and how will findings benefit the product?

2. Determine participant profile — age, gender, behaviours, number of participants per profile.

3. Screen and shortlist candidates — screener questions to qualify or disqualify candidates. Get consent to participate and be recorded.

4. Choose a platform — check it supports your research method, test materials and target devices.

5. Gather test materials — wireframes or prototypes. Include NDAs if needed.

6. Create tasks — limit to 5-7 tasks. Each must match study objectives, be clearly worded, have defined success criteria and a clear end state so participants know when they're done.

7. Define usability metrics per task:

  • Task success or failure
  • Time to complete
  • Time to first click or tap
  • Number of clicks, taps, swipes
  • Navigation paths taken
  • Number of retries

8. Plan post-task questions — Likert scale, multiple choice, ranking, open-ended. Ask about task difficulty and recall.

9. Do a dry run — test it yourself first, then with internal and external participants. Check prototypes load correctly. Fix anything before launch.
