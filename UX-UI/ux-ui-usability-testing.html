Lesson 32 — Usability Testing

Usability testing is the process of getting real people to use a product you've designed while tracking their actions and reactions. Users are given tasks to complete while a researcher observes where they struggle or get confused. If multiple people hit the same problem, that's a clear signal something needs fixing.

The three players

The facilitator gives participants their tasks, monitors their behaviour, listens to feedback and asks follow-up questions to dig deeper into any issues that come up.

The participant is a real user of the product or a similar one. They carry out practical tasks that reflect everyday actions they'd actually perform.

The tasks are the specific actions participants are asked to complete. Task wording is critical — even small inaccuracies can cause participants to misinterpret what they need to do, which skews your results. Tasks can be read aloud by the facilitator or handed to participants as written task sheets. Participants are often asked to think aloud as they work so the facilitator can follow their reasoning.

Types of usability testing

Qualitative usability testing gathers insights, narratives and observations about how users interact with a product. It's the most effective method for identifying UX problems and is the most commonly used type. The Nielsen Norman Group recommends five participants for typical qualitative research on a single user group — enough to surface most usability issues.

Quantitative usability testing collects measurable metrics — task success rate and time on task are the two most common. It's better suited for benchmarking than for identifying specific problems.

In-person vs online

In-person testing has the facilitator and participant in the same room. Online testing is more popular because it costs less time and money.

Moderated online testing works similarly to in-person — the facilitator assigns tasks and speaks with the participant remotely, usually via a screen-sharing app.

Unmoderated online testing has no facilitator interaction at all. Tasks are set up in advance using remote testing software. The participant works alone at their own pace and the session recording plus metrics are sent to the researcher when done.

Key takeaway

Five participants, qualitative, moderated — that's the starting point for most usability tests. Watch where people get stuck, listen to what they say while they work, and use that to inform the next iteration of your design.
