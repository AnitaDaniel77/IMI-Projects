Lesson 8 — What is UI?

To interface with something means to interact or communicate with it. You interface with humans, computers and applications every day — emailing a colleague, changing printer settings, ordering food from an app.

What is a UI?

A graphical user interface (GUI) presents interactive visual components people can interact with on-screen to communicate with technology. Everything you see and tap is the UI:

  • Information and how it is laid out
  • Icons, colours, text, shapes
  • What happens when you tap or click something

Real life examples

A car dashboard is a perfect UI example. Press the gas pedal — that is an input. The speedometer reacts and the car goes faster — that is feedback. Input and feedback is exactly how digital UIs work.

Checking Wi-Fi signal and battery level on your phone — that is communication through a UI. A medical device displaying vitals to a doctor — that is a UI where good design can save lives.

Brief history of UI

  • Cave drawings — sticks used to illustrate stories. The earliest human interface.
  • The quill became the pen. The pen became the keyboard — derived from the typewriter. Same interaction: fingers on keys to assemble words.
  • 1981 — Xerox Star: first PC with a graphical user interface. Introduced design metaphors still in use today — the desktop, windows, menus, and icons. These helped users by linking familiar mental models to an unfamiliar digital space.
  • Smartphones and tablets: introduced multi-touch screens and gesture-based interaction. This phase brought UI to where it is today — everywhere, from ATMs to electric vehicle dashboards.

Why good UI matters

The intuitiveness of a UI design can save lives — think airplane controls in a storm or a medical device communicating vitals in an emergency. In everyday products, a poor UI costs you users. A well-designed one keeps them coming back.

Key takeaway

UI is everything a user sees and interacts with. Its history shows that the best interfaces borrow from familiar mental models — the desktop metaphor worked because people already understood a physical desk. When you design for Little Lemon, the same principle applies: use patterns users already know so they spend zero time learning and all their time ordering.
